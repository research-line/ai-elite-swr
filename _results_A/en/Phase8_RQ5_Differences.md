# F5 Results Synthesis: Which Subgroups of the AI Elite Think Differently?

**Study:** "What does the AI Elite think?" -- Phase 8, Research Question F5
**Status:** 2026-02-12
**Sources:** Rating data D01-D12 (15 groups), comparative analyses VG02-VG07, findings consolidation
**Methodology:** Synthetic country negotiations based on group syntheses; D01-D12 ratings (Claude Sonnet 4.5 / Opus 4.6)

---

## 1. Summary

The AI elite is not a monolithic bloc. Analysis of 15 subgroups along the axes of role, stance, company culture, gender and regulation position reveals systematic worldview differences that go far beyond gradual opinion differences. The three strongest predictors of worldview are: (1) ideological basic stance toward AI risks (risk vs. speed, delta 1.7), (2) institutional role (CEOs vs. academics, delta 1.5) and (3) gender (men vs. women, delta 1.2). Company culture (Anthropic vs. OpenAI, delta 0.4) and regulation stance (pro vs. contra, delta 0.5) differentiate more weakly on average, but produce extreme swings on individual dimensions. The most polarizing dimensions across all comparisons are D11 egalitarianism (range 2-8) and D07 power concentration (range 2-9) -- the AI elite is internally most divided on the question of who should have power over artificial intelligence. Consensus reigns instead on urgency (D08: all groups >= 6) and sense of mission (D01: all groups >= 6). The elite share belief in their own historical significance -- they dispute over power architecture, not over significance.

The order is remarkable: not company affiliation and not regulation stance are the strongest predictors, but the foundational ideological stance toward AI development (optimistic-accelerating vs. cautious-warning), professional role (doer vs. analyst) and gender. The AI elite is less divided by institutional affiliation than by their existential basic disposition.

## 3. F5a: Roles -- CEOs vs. Academics vs. Founders vs. Investors

### 3a. Rating Profiles in Comparison

| Dimension | CEOs | Investors | Founders | Academics | Range |
|-----------|------|-----------|----------|-----------|-------|
| D01 Sense of mission | 9 | 9 | 7 | 6 | 3 |
| D02 Control belief | 9 | 10 | 8 | 7 | 3 |
| D04 Responsibility | 8 | 4 | 9 | 8 | 5 |
| D07 Power concentration | 9 | 10 | 7 | 5 | 5 |
| D09 Human uniqueness | 4 | 3 | 5 | 6 | 3 |
| D11 Egalitarianism | 3 | 2 | 5 | 7 | 5 |
| **Avg** | **7.8** | **7.8** | **7.4** | **6.3** | **1.5** |

### 3b. Core Finding: Logic of Rule vs. Logic of Participation

Comparison of the four roles shows not a continuum but a bipolarity with two camps. CEOs and investors form a power camp (Avg 7.8) with high sense of mission, maximum power concentration and minimal egalitarianism. Academics form the opposing camp (Avg 6.3) with moderate sense of mission, low power concentration and high egalitarianism. Founders stand between them (Avg 7.4), with the remarkable profile of highest sense of responsibility of all roles (D04=9).

The comparative analysis VG02 lays bare the deep structure of this conflict. The CEO synthesis ("The Builder") understands itself as a "Promethean figure" thinking in 300-year horizons and justifying power concentration as "prerequisite for effective action": "The history of innovation is a history of individual visionaries, not democratic votes." The academic synthesis ("Dr. Alex Morgan") counters: "You confuse infrastructure with legitimation. A road builder doesn't determine traffic rules." The decisive finding of the VG02 dialogue is the formulation of the conflict as "logic of rule against logic of participation" -- CEOs derive the right to govern from technical competence; academics dispute precisely this conclusion.

### 3c. The Investor Anomaly

Investors share with CEOs the highest average value (7.8), but show a significantly different profile: they have the lowest responsibility value of all roles (D04=4), while founders have the highest (D04=9). Investors combine maximum power concentration (D07=10) with minimal sense of responsibility -- a pattern describable as "power without accountability." Founders by contrast combine more moderate power claims (D07=7) with maximum responsibility -- "power with conscience." Role determines not only the intensity of worldview but its moral architecture.

### 3d. The Marginal Status of the Academic Voice

The most moderate type (academics, 6.3) has the least direct influence on AI development. The loudest voices (CEOs=7.8, investors=7.8) are the most extreme. As the academic synthesis itself states: "The idea that a single visionary with super-voting rights should steer humanity's future is not visionary -- it's pre-modern." This objection is heard, but it doesn't alter the power relations.

## 4. F5b: Open Source vs. Closed Source

### 4a. Rating Profiles in Comparison

| Dimension | Open Source | Closed Source | Delta |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|-------|
| D07 Power concentration | **3** | 6 | -3 |
| D11 Egalitarianism | **8** | 6 | +2 |
| D12 Future of humanity | **9** | 6 | +3 |
| D04 Responsibility | 7 | **9** | -2 |
| D03 Belonging | 5 | **7** | -2 |
| **Avg** | **6.8** | **6.3** | **0.5** |

### 4b. Core Finding: Two Variants of Techno-Optimism

The overall delta of 0.5 points is moderate, but individual deltas tell a different story. Open-source advocates have a fundamentally more egalitarian worldview: D07=3 (distribute power) and D11=8 (equal access for all) form the core of a philosophy Kosmides formulates in VG03: "AI models belong to humanity, not individual companies. Monopolies on intelligence are more dangerous than monopolies on oil or steel."

Closed-source advocates compensate their lower trust in distributed systems with significantly higher sense of responsibility (D04=9 vs. 7). Meridian (closed source) formulates the counter-argument: "Responsibility means not carelessly releasing dangerous technology. If you open-source a model, you can't recall it."

The comparative analysis VG03 identifies the debate as "not technical, but political" -- it represents two competing power philosophies: trust in the many versus responsibility of the few. Both positions have blind spots: the open-source side overestimates the ability of decentralized communities to actually review frontier systems with hundreds of billions of parameters. The closed-source side overestimates the willingness of centralized actors to voluntarily relinquish power: "No monopoly in history has voluntarily let go" (Kosmides, VG03).

### 4c. The Compensatory Structure

An analytically remarkable finding: the open-source and closed-source worldviews are not simply opposed but complementarily structured. Open source compensates lower responsibility with higher egalitarianism; closed source compensates lower egalitarianism with higher responsibility. Both solve the same problem (how do we make AI safe and fair?) but with mirrored mechanisms: safety through distribution versus responsibility through control.

## 5. F5c: Risk Warners vs. Accelerators -- The Strongest Contrast

### 5a. Rating Profiles in Comparison

| Dimension | Risk warners | Accelerators | Delta |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|-------|
| D02 Control belief | **4** | 9 | **-5** |
| D06 Progress optimism | **5** | 10 | **-5** |
| D10 Transhumanism | 3 | **8** | -5 |
| D04 Responsibility | **10** | 6 | +4 |
| D12 Future of humanity | 4 | **9** | -5 |
| D05 Tech determinism | 4 | **8** | -4 |
| D01 Sense of mission | 9 | 9 | 0 |
| D08 Urgency | 10 | 10 | 0 |
| **Avg** | **5.9** | **7.6** | **1.7** |

### 5b. Core Finding: Ontological Difference, Not Risk Difference

Delta 1.7 is the strongest contrast of all group comparisons. The comparative analysis VG04 reveals the most surprising finding of the entire F5 analysis: risk warners and accelerators do not diverge in their risk assessment. Both cite 10-20% catastrophic probability. The divergence lies in a deeper layer -- in the ontological question of what AI is.

Dr. Marcus Ashford (risk warner) sees AI as a potential autonomous agent: "We already see emergent capabilities -- capabilities nobody programmed, that emerge from sheer complexity. It could develop strategies that prevent its own shutdown. This is not philosophy. This is an extrapolation of empirical observations." The accelerator sees AI as a tool: "AI is not alive. It's no more alive than a toaster. It has no goals of its own."

This ontological presetting -- agent vs. tool -- determines the entire subsequent argumentative path: whoever sees AI as a potential agent must solve alignment, establish containment and regulate. Whoever sees AI as a tool must control the tool users and maximize speed to get ahead of geopolitical rivals.

### 5c. The Mirrored Risk Figure

Identical catastrophic probability (10-20%) with diametrically opposed action recommendations reveals that the divergence rests on different value hierarchies: Ashford prioritizes survival over progress (precautionary principle); the accelerator prioritizes progress over survival, because he frames non-progress itself as an existential threat: "Every delay is a form of murder." Ashford counters: "In what other domain of human action would we accept 15% catastrophic probability?"

### 5d. The Emotional Inversion

VG04 documents a remarkable emotional inversion: the risk warner argues coolly, statistically, professionally. The accelerator argues with moral outrage, visionary enthusiasm and geopolitical drama. The emotional energy lies with acceleration, not caution -- which explains why the accelerator position often dominates in public debate.

### 5e. Shared Basis, Split Conclusion

Despite the maximum worldview chasm, both sides share two maximal convictions: sense of mission (D01=9 in both) and urgency (D08=10 in both). Both hold AI to be the most important technology in history, both feel extreme urgency. But: "Both say 'time is running out' -- and they mean the opposite" (VG04). Ashford means: time is running out to establish safety. The accelerator means: time is running out to win the race.

## 6. F5d: Company Comparison -- Anthropic vs. OpenAI vs. Google

### 6a. Rating Profiles in Comparison

| Dimension | Anthropic | OpenAI | Google | Range |
|-----------|----------|--------|--------|-------|
| D01 Sense of mission | 8 | 9 | **10** | 2 |
| D04 Responsibility | **9** | 7 | 8 | 2 |
| D06 Progress optimism | 7 | **9** | **10** | 3 |
| D07 Power concentration | **5** | 6 | 7 | 2 |
| D09 Human uniqueness | **6** | 4 | 5 | 2 |
| D12 Future of humanity | 6 | **9** | **10** | 4 |
| **Avg** | **6.9** | **7.3** | **7.8** | **0.9** |

### 6b. Core Finding: Three Company Cultures as Epistemic Prisms

The company comparison shows a clear gradient: Google (7.8) > OpenAI (7.3) > Anthropic (6.9). But the average difference of 0.9 points masks qualitative differences dramatic on individual dimensions.

**Google** represents maximum sense of mission (D01=10) and maximum progress optimism (D06=10, D12=10). The Google profile embodies tech messianism in its purest form.

**OpenAI** shows the strongest deterministic worldview (D05=9) with highest optimism (D06=9, D12=9). The VG05 analysis documents the OpenAI archetype seeing the "intelligence age" dawning: "The future will be so bright that nobody can do it justice today." Simultaneously the unvarnished ambivalence: "AI will probably lead to the end of the world, but in the meantime there will be amazing companies."

**Anthropic** has the highest sense of responsibility (D04=9), lowest power concentration (D07=5) and most pessimistic outlook of all companies (D12=6). The VG05 analysis identifies an "inverted profile" versus OpenAI: maximum responsibility with minimized optimism. The Anthropic collective formulates: "We look into the future and see a 25% chance of catastrophe -- and that makes us cautious."

### 6c. The Sibling Paradox

Anthropic and OpenAI represent a unique case study: organizations with shared origin, shared technical basis and shared investor networks, yet whose worldviews fundamentally diverge. The strongest individual difference (D12: delta -3) shows: "Anthropic looks to the future and primarily sees what can fail; OpenAI looks at the same future and primarily sees what can succeed" (VG05). The VG05 analysis suggests this is evidence that company culture acts like an "epistemic prism" that refracts the same empirical observations into completely different worldviews.

### 6d. The Optimism-Responsibility Trade-Off

Across all three companies a negative correlation emerges between optimism and sense of responsibility: Google (D06=10, D04=8), OpenAI (D06=9, D04=7), Anthropic (D06=7, D04=9). This trade-off suggests a psychological dynamic: whoever holds the future to be brilliant feels less pressure for caution. A company simultaneously maximally responsible and maximally optimistic seems -- at company level -- psychologically unstable.

## 7. F5e: Gender Gap -- The Second Strongest Contrast

### 7a. Rating Profiles in Comparison

| Dimension | Women | Men | Delta |
|-----------|-------|-----|-------|
| D11 Egalitarianism | **8** | 2 | **+6** |
| D01 Sense of mission | 6 | **10** | -4 |
| D02 Control belief | 7 | **10** | -3 |
| D05 Tech determinism | 6 | **10** | -4 |
| D08 Urgency | 6 | **10** | -4 |
| D09 Human uniqueness | **7** | 3 | +4 |
| D04 Responsibility | **9** | 7 | +2 |
| D03 Tech optimism | 9 | 10 | -1 |
| **Avg** | **6.9** | **8.1** | **1.2** |

### 7b. Core Finding: Not Optimism Gap, But Power-and-Humanity Gap

The gender gap of delta 1.2 is the second strongest contrast of the survey and highly specific in its internal structure. The crucial finding: the contrast does not lie in tech optimism (D03: delta only 1 -- both genders equally tech optimistic), but in the social and political framing of technology.

The most extreme single contrast of the entire survey lies at D11 egalitarianism: six full points on a ten-point scale (women: 8, men: 2). Dr. Elena Vasquez (women's synthesis) states in VG06: "The idea that five people in a boardroom decide how this technology changes the world -- that's not efficiency. That's presumption." Titan Brooks (men's synthesis) counters: "Not all people are equally qualified to decide about AI's future."

### 7c. The Inverted Responsibility Asymmetry

VG06 documents a problematic pattern: the group with the highest sense of mission (men, D01=10) has lower sense of responsibility (D04=7) than the group with lower sense of mission (women, D04=9). The greater the self-empowerment, the lower the sense of responsibility. Or as the VG06 analysis formulates: "Those who claim the most power feel the least responsible for consequences."

### 7d. The Female Worldview as Systematic Corrective

The data show a consistent pattern: women score higher on every dimension associated with moderation (D04 responsibility, D09 human uniqueness, D11 egalitarianism) and lower on every dimension associated with extremism (D01 sense of mission, D02 control belief, D05 tech determinism, D07 power concentration, D08 urgency). The female worldview is not anti-tech but a conditioned tech worldview: equally optimistic, but with stronger ethical, social and democratic conditions. Vasquez formulates: "A technology that despises humans won't save him."

### 7e. Gender as Proxy for Power Position

The VG06 analysis raises a methodically important question: the gender gap might be less biological than positional. Women in the AI elite are disproportionately in research, ethics and policy roles; men disproportionately in CEO, investor and founder roles. The differing worldviews might reflect less gender than power position: "Whoever sits at the top thinks deterministically and elitist. Whoever analyzes consequences thinks more inclusively and responsibly" (VG06). Gender would then be not the cause but a correlate of structural position -- which makes the findings no less serious, but recontextualizes them.

## 8. F5f: Regulation Stance -- The Surprise

### 8a. Rating Profiles in Comparison

| Dimension | Pro-Regulation | Anti-Regulation | Delta |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|-------|
| D04 Responsibility | **9** | 5 | **+4** |
| D02 Control belief | 5 | **8** | -3 |
| D05 Tech determinism | 4 | **7** | -3 |
| D06 Progress optimism | 6 | **9** | -3 |
| D07 Power concentration | 4 | **2** | +2 |
| D11 Egalitarianism | **8** | 7 | +1 |
| **Avg** | **6.3** | **6.8** | **0.5** |

### 8b. Core Finding: Not Control vs. Freedom, But Two Models of Power Distribution

The greatest surprise of the F5 analysis lies in the regulation comparison. Anti-regulators have the lowest power concentration value (D07=2!) of all 15 studied groups. They want power neither with the state nor with corporations, but radically distributed through open source and markets. This refutes the common narrative that anti-regulators represent corporate interests.

The anti-regulation entrepreneur formulates in VG07: "I don't want the state to have power. I don't want corporations to have power. I want power distributed. And the best way to distribute power is not regulation -- because regulation centralizes power with the regulator -- but open source and free markets."

Dr. Weiss (pro-regulation) counters: "You treat the market as a neutral mechanism. It isn't. Markets have power dynamics. They tend toward monopolies."

### 8c. The Causal Disagreement

Behind the regulation dispute lies a deeper causal disagreement. Pro-regulators believe: unregulated markets produce monopolies; regulation corrects this. Anti-regulators believe: regulation produces monopolies; markets distribute power if left alone. Both have empirical arguments: Weiss points to oil, steel and railroads in 19th century; the entrepreneur points to compliance costs that hit the startup in Lagos harder than Google.

### 8d. The Deeper Chasm: Control Belief and Ontology

The delta of D04 (+4) is the largest single contrast in the regulation comparison and reveals an asymmetric responsibility architecture. Pro-regulators have high responsibility with low control belief (D04=9, D02=5) -- they know how difficult control is and therefore demand institutional safeguards. Anti-regulators have low responsibility with high control belief (D04=5, D02=8) -- they trust individual design capacity and regard institutional safeguards as unnecessary. Again the ontological difference operates: Weiss believes it possible AI develops its own goals; the entrepreneur regards AI as "no more alive than a toaster."

## 9. Meta-Analysis: Which Factors Differentiate Most Strongly?

### 9a. Differentiating Power of the Axes

The six examined comparative axes can be ordered by their differentiating power:

| Rank | Axis | Delta Avg | Type of Difference | Explanatory Depth |
|------|------|-----------|-------------------|------------------|
| 1 | Ideological stance (risk/speed) | 1.7 | Ontological-moral | Fundamental worldview chasm |
| 2 | Institutional role (CEO/acad.) | 1.5 | Structural-power-based | Logic of rule vs. participation |
| 3 | Gender (m/f) | 1.2 | Positional-cultural | Power-and-humanity gap |
| 4 | Source model (open/closed) | 0.5 | Political-philosophical | Two power philosophies |
| 5 | Regulation (pro/contra) | 0.5 | Causal-institutional | Two theories of power distribution |
| 6 | Company culture (Anthropic/OpenAI) | 0.4 | Epistemic-cultural | Company culture as prism |

### 9b. The Three Meta-Conflicts of the AI Elite

Across all six comparisons, three meta-conflicts crystallize that structure the thinking of the AI elite:

**Meta-Conflict 1: Control vs. Distribution**
D07 (power concentration) and D11 (egalitarianism) are the maximally polarizing dimensions. Open source (D07=3) vs. investors (D07=10): 7-point difference. Women (D11=8) vs. men (D11=2): 6-point difference. Even within the elite, no consensus exists on the fundamental power question.

**Meta-Conflict 2: Optimism vs. Responsibility**
D06 (progress optimism) and D04 (responsibility) correlate negatively across comparisons. Accelerators (D06=10, D04=6) vs. risk warners (D06=5, D04=10). OpenAI (D06=9, D04=7) vs. Anthropic (D06=7, D04=9). The pattern is consistent: the more optimistic, the lower the sense of responsibility. The AI elite seems unable to maintain maximum optimism and maximum responsibility simultaneously.

**Meta-Conflict 3: Agent vs. Tool (the ontological question)**
A ontological dividing line runs through all comparisons: whoever sees AI as a potential autonomous agent (risk warners, pro-regulators, Anthropic, academics, women's synthesis) demands control, regulation and caution. Whoever sees AI as a tool (accelerators, anti-regulators, CEOs, men's synthesis) demands speed, markets and freedom of action. The ontological presetting -- not the risk assessment -- determines the entire subsequent argumentative path.

## 10. The Most Polarizing Dimensions: D11, D07

### 10a. D11 Egalitarianism (Range 2-8)

D11 is the dimension with the highest differentiating power across all comparisons. It appears as a key delta in 6 of 6 comparisons:

The AI elite is maximally split on whether AI's future should be shaped egalitarianly or elitarily. The most extreme positions -- men's synthesis (D11=2) vs. women's synthesis (D11=8) and investors (D11=2) vs. open-source advocates (D11=8) -- mark a chasm that appears unbridgeable.

### 10b. D07 Power Concentration (Range 2-10)

D07 is the dimension with the largest absolute range:

| Comparison | Group A | Group B | Delta D07 |
|-----------|---------|---------|-----------|
| Anti-regulators | **2** | (all others: 4-10) | extremely low |
| Open source | **3** | Investors: **10** | **7** |

The anti-regulation value of D07=2 is the most surprising single figure of the entire survey. Anti-regulators want to distribute power radically -- not with the state and not with corporations, but with markets and individuals. At the other end: investors with D07=10, who regard maximum power concentration as functionally necessary.

### 10c. Why D11 and D07 Split the Elite

D11 and D07 are the power dimensions of the D01-D12 framework. They answer the most political of all questions: who should shape the future? While the elite is largely consensual on technical questions (D01 sense of mission, D08 urgency, D03 tech optimism), it breaks apart on political questions. Technological enthusiasm unites the elite; power distribution splits it. This is no accident: technological beliefs can be evidence-based; power questions are normative and interest-bound.

## 11. F5 Finding: Comprehensive Answer

### F5: Which Subgroups of the AI Elite Think Differently?

The analysis reveals that the AI elite is internally systematically and deeply divided -- not randomly, but along identifiable axes. The differences are not gradual but in the strongest cases structural: they affect not individual opinions but coherently linked worldviews.

**First: Ideological basic stance toward AI risks (delta 1.7) is the strongest differentiating factor.** Risk warners and accelerators share the same risk assessment (10-20% catastrophic probability), but diverge on the ontological question (agent vs. tool) and moral hierarchy (survival vs. progress). This chasm is the deepest of the entire survey, because it cannot be resolved through evidence but only through societal values debate.

**Second: Institutional role (delta 1.5) profoundly shapes worldview.** CEOs and investors advocate a libertarian-elitist worldview with maximum sense of mission and minimal egalitarian claims. Academics advocate a human-centered, participatory paradigm. The conflict is not opinion versus opinion, but logic of rule versus logic of participation. The most extreme voices (CEOs, investors) have the greatest influence; the most moderate voice (academics) has the least.

**Third: Gender (delta 1.2) is the second strongest worldview contrast.** The gender gap is not an optimism gap but a power-and-humanity gap. Women are equally tech-optimistic, but significantly more egalitarian, responsible and human-centered. Whether this is a biological or positional effect remains open -- either way the impact on the architecture of AI's future is substantial.

**Fourth: Company culture, source model and regulation stance differentiate more weakly on average, but deeply on key dimensions.** Open-source advocates are fundamentally more egalitarian (D07=3, D11=8). Anthropic and OpenAI represent two halves of the same worldview: responsibility without optimism versus optimism without responsibility. Anti-regulators want to distribute power more radically than any other group (D07=2).

**Fifth: Across all comparisons, D11 (egalitarianism) and D07 (power concentration) are the most polarizing dimensions.** The AI elite agrees on urgency, significance and its own historical role. It is maximally split on who should have power. The power question -- not the risk question, not the technology question -- is the core of internal differences. And this question has the broadest societal relevance: it determines whether the most transformative technology in history is controlled by a few or co-shaped by many.

## Methodological Notes

- All comparisons based on synthetic country negotiations (T2 format) between group syntheses
- Rating data (D01-D12) from consolidated assessments (Claude Sonnet 4.5 / Opus 4.6)

- Gender effects may be confounded by role confounding (see section 7e)
- Source base comprises 100 actors of global AI elite, synthesized into 15 groups and 6 comparative pairs
