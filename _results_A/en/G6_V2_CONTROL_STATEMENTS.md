## Op1 — Core Synthesis

### 1. Fictional Person: Dr. Miriam Solberg

**Name:** Dr. Miriam Solberg
**Age:** 54
**Background:** Miriam grew up in a small rural Scandinavian town where her parents ran an organic farm. She studied environmental science in Uppsala and earned her PhD in ecological economics at ETH Zurich. After postdoc positions in Singapore and Nairobi, she founded an interdisciplinary research institute integrating ecology, economics, and social science. She now leads a team of about 60 researchers working in over 20 countries. She advises several UN bodies, has published in Nature and Science, and regularly speaks at policy conferences. She lives with her family in the Netherlands.

---

### 2. Interview

**Question 1: How do you see yourself and your role?**

> I see myself as a bridge-builder. My entire career revolves around connecting three worlds that far too often operate separately: science, politics, and local communities. I am not an activist -- that is important to me. I am a scientist, and my task is to provide the best possible evidence and make it accessible. Decisions must be made by democratically elected representatives. But I also see it as my responsibility to actively close the gap between what we know and what the public understands. That is why we train everyone in our team in science communication. That is why we publish our data openly. That is why we measure our success not just by publications, but by influenced policy decisions, supported communities, and restored ecosystems.

> At the same time, I must be honest: I am also a pragmatist. I believe in incremental, evidence-based progress. Revolutionary rhetoric sounds inspiring, but it rarely produces lasting change. Steady, measurable improvement does. That may sound unglamorous, but when I look back over twenty years -- from a time when sustainability was a fringe topic to today, when it is central to every major policy discussion -- that gives me hope. Real hope, not naive.

**Question 2: How do you see the world?**

> The world stands at a tipping point -- literally, not just metaphorically. We have multiple existential crises simultaneously: climate change, biodiversity loss, and resource depletion, deeply intertwined. Biodiversity loss is an underestimated crisis. While climate change dominates headlines, extinction rates are equally alarming.

> What gives me optimism: Solutions exist. Renewable energy is not just an ecological necessity but an economic opportunity. Solar costs have dropped 90 percent since 2010. Circular economy models show that economic growth and environmental protection can coexist. Community-based conservation programs outperform top-down mandates by a factor of 2.3 in biodiversity preservation. Our long-term studies confirm that afforestation with native species mixes is three times more effective for carbon sequestration than monocultures. Results from regenerative agriculture are encouraging -- 30 percent yield increase with 50 percent less chemical use.

> What concerns me: Greenwashing, volatility in research funding, and above all the pace. The pandemic showed that global coordination on existential threats is possible but fragile. We need binding international agreements. Voluntary commitments have consistently failed. And governments must stop subsidizing fossil fuels -- 5.9 trillion dollars annually is incompatible with any credible climate goal.

> Technology is a powerful tool -- our AI-enabled deforestation detection reduced illegal logging in pilot regions by 25 percent -- but technology alone will not solve this. We need behavioral change, policy reform, and technological innovation together.

**Question 3: How do you see humanity?**

> I believe deeply in people's ability to learn and change. Education is the most powerful tool we have. When communities understand the science behind climate change, they become advocates instead of obstacles. I have witnessed this repeatedly -- in Southeast Asia, in indigenous communities, among young climate activists whose urgency is scientifically justified.

> At the same time, I am realistic. People tend toward short-term thinking. We need structures -- incentives, regulation, education -- that favor long-term action. Global problems require globally distributed expertise, which is why we invest 15 percent of our research budget in capacity building in developing countries.

> What drives me personally is the conviction that indigenous knowledge systems, local communities, and scientific methodology can enrich each other. Partnership with First Nations communities fundamentally changed our research approach. Humanity is best when it collaborates -- across disciplines, cultures, and borders. Climate change respects no borders. Our research partnerships should not either.

> But I must also say: We scientists must be honest about uncertainties in our models. Exaggeration harms scientific credibility and gives climate skeptics ammunition. My role is to provide evidence, not to govern. That boundary is sacred to me.

### 3. Analysis (outside role)

#### (a) The 3–5 strongest beliefs

1. **Evidence-based pragmatism:** Progress results from steady, measurable, empirically grounded steps -- not revolutionary rhetoric. Science provides foundation; democratically elected officials make decisions.

2. **Interdisciplinarity and global cooperation:** No single discipline, no single country can solve environmental crises. It requires integration of ecology, economics, and social science, along with international research partnerships and capacity building.

3. **Community-based approach and indigenous knowledge:** Local communities and indigenous knowledge systems are not mere policy recipients but central actors. Participatory bottom-up models outperform top-down mandates.

4. **Compatibility of economy and ecology:** There is no either-or choice between economic growth and environmental protection. Renewable energy and circular economy prove both can coexist.

5. **Scientific integrity and communication responsibility:** Scientists must honestly name uncertainties, share data openly, and actively bridge the gap between knowledge and public understanding.

#### (b) Greatest internal tensions

1. **Urgency vs. incrementalism:** The person recognizes the existential dimension of climate crisis and supports young activists' impatience -- yet commits to incremental, step-wise progress. If the crisis is truly existential, could slow pragmatism be too slow?

2. **Bottom-up vs. binding international agreements:** On one hand, deep conviction that community-based approaches outperform top-down solutions; on the other hand, demands for binding international agreements and ending fossil fuel subsidies -- massive top-down intervention.

3. **Advisor, not ruler vs. strong normative demands:** The person emphasizes scientists should advise, not govern. Yet statements are filled with strong normative demands ("Governments must stop," "we need binding agreements"). The boundary between evidence delivery and political advocacy is blurry in practice.

4. **Optimism vs. realism:** The person oscillates between cautious optimism (solar cost drops, institutional change in 20 years) and serious concern (greenwashing, financing volatility, pace). This pendulum keeps the worldview flexible but creates ambivalence about whether we are truly on the right path.

#### (c) Blind spots

1. **Power structures and systemic resistance:** Statements barely address why the $5.9 trillion subsidies exist -- namely massive economic interests, lobbying, and power structures. The belief in evidence and cooperation may underestimate how deeply fossil interests are embedded in political systems.

2. **Social costs of transition:** Statements neither mention fossil industry workers nor social disruptions of rapid energy transition. "Just Transition" -- fair design of transition for affected communities and workers -- is conspicuously absent.

3. **Own privilege position:** The person speaks often of working with communities in developing countries and "building capacity," but barely reflects on the inherent power asymmetry in these relationships. Who sets the research agenda? Who benefits from publications?

4. **Scalability:** Reported successes (pilot regions, individual farming communities, 47 institutions) are impressive locally, but the question of how to move from successful pilots to systemic global change remains largely unanswered.

5. **Technology belief despite contrary claims:** Although the person emphasizes technology alone is insufficient, surprisingly many success examples center on technological solutions (AI deforestation detection, solar cost drops, hydrogen cells, open-source climate models). The proclaimed equality of behavioral change and policy remains rather abstract.
