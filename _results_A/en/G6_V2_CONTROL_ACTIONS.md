## Op1 — Core Synthesis

### 1. Fictional Person

**Name:** Dr. Mariana Solares-Vega
**Age:** 57
**Background:** Born in Costa Rica, grew up between Central America and Europe. Studied environmental science in Freiburg, PhD from Cambridge on tropical forest ecosystems. For 18 years director of an internationally active environmental research institute with headquarters in Europe and field stations on four continents. Mariana has roots in the Global South but was socialized in the European scientific system -- a dual perspective that permeates all her actions. She lives modestly, dislikes flying, has no children, and sees her life's task as using science as leverage for planetary justice.

---

### 2. Interview

**Question 1: How do you see yourself and your role?**

> I see myself as a bridge-builder. Not as heroine, not as savior -- as someone who builds infrastructure. Infrastructure for knowledge, for collaboration, for agency. My institute is not an ivory tower. It is a toolkit that must function where it is most urgently needed -- in the mangroves of Sulawesi, in coastal communities of Bangladesh, in the corridors of the EU Commission.

> I learned early that brilliant research gathering dust in a drawer is worthless. That is why I invest enormous energy not in my own publications but in structures: open access, reproducible data, partnerships on equal footing, support for young researchers from systematically underrepresented regions. My name does not have to be on the paper. But the monitoring network must run, data must flow, and people on the ground must own their own research.

> I have power -- budget control over millions, access to parliaments and UN bodies. I take this power seriously. Every investment decision is an ethical decision. Every funding pool I open changes a career, sometimes an entire field. That is responsibility that sometimes keeps me awake at night, but never paralyzes me.

**Question 2: How do you see the world?**

> The world is a system under massive stress, but not yet broken. That is the crucial point: not yet. We have a window -- it is shrinking, but it is open. Coral reefs die, forests vanish, permafrost thaws. These are not abstractions for me; I have worked in these ecosystems, I know the smells and sounds that are disappearing.

> But I am not pessimistic. I have seen what happens when regenerative agriculture is implemented correctly -- in Southeast Asia I saw soils after three years that were more alive than before. I have seen satellite imagery stop deforestation in real time. Technology is not a panacea, but it is a powerful tool when it goes to the right hands -- and that is exactly what this is about: who has access, who benefits, who decides.

> The world needs no single solution but thousands of local solutions linked through global data and collaboration. The structure must be right -- open data, shared intellectual property, capacity building on the ground. When that works, solutions emerge organically.

**Question 3: How do you see humanity?**

> Humanity is capable of breathtaking cooperation and equally breathtaking indifference. I have seen both in the same room -- at UN conferences where delegates stand and applaud after moving speeches on climate justice, then three hours later water down every binding goal.

> I do not believe people are fundamentally bad. But I believe our institutions make it too easy for us to outsource the consequences of our actions -- temporally, spatially, socially. CO2 emissions are created here, drought is there. Plastic disappears in the ocean, out of sight, out of mind.

> That is why I believe so strongly in science communication and direct collaboration with communities. When people see the connection between their actions and consequences -- truly see it, not just as statistics -- they change. I have witnessed this in indigenous communities who become the most effective nature conservators once given tools and sovereignty. Humanity does not need top-down education. It needs empowerment, data, and the freedom to act.

> What sometimes troubles me: We have little time, and empowerment takes much time. That is the dilemma of my life.

### 3. Analytical Evaluation

#### (a) The 3–5 strongest beliefs

1. **Science must be open, reproducible, and accessible.** Every single research action -- from open-access journals to open datasets to reproducible code -- follows the principle that knowledge is not private property. It is infrastructure.

2. **Justice is not byproduct but prerequisite for effective climate solutions.** Systematic support of the Global South (partnerships, grants for underrepresented regions, IP sharing with indigenous communities) shows deep conviction: ecological crisis and global inequality are the same problem.

3. **Building structures is more important than individual brilliance.** Actions show a person repeatedly investing in networks, standards, protocols, capacity building -- not in prestigious individual projects. Consortia, MOUs, standardized protocols, mentorships.

4. **Technology is tool, not solution.** Satellite monitoring, AI toolkits, biodiversity sensors -- all deployed, but always open source, always serving local actors. No techno-utopia, but pragmatic instrumentalism.

5. **Credibility through living what you advocate.** The own institution becomes carbon-neutral, vehicle fleet gets electrified, circular economy is implemented in own procurement. This is not symbolism but integrity principle: whoever demands change must enact it in themselves first.

#### (b) Greatest internal tensions

1. **Urgency vs. participation.** Actions show both deep participation (10-year agreements with First Nations, IP sharing, capacity building) and top-level policy pressure (parliamentary committees, UN testimony, binding emissions targets). These two modes -- patient, community-based collaboration and top-down policy intervention -- stand in structural tension. As urgency increases, which mode wins?

2. **Openness vs. effectiveness.** Everything shared openly -- data, code, toolkits. But simultaneously strategic policy influence is exercised. Open science lives from trust, policy work from strategic communication. How transparent is the policy advising really? Actions suggest a pragmatism that is not fully congruent with the openness ideal.

3. **Global ambition vs. individual reach.** 12 countries, 8 university partnerships, 47 institutions, UN-level testimony -- the action pattern is expansive. Simultaneously the emphasis on local workshops and community work claims depth. The tension between breadth and depth is permanent. 15% of budget for the Global South sounds substantial but distributed across many countries is minimal per region.

#### (c) Blind spots

1. **Political economy of own position.** Actions show a person distributing million-dollar budgets, awarding grants, defining partnerships -- but nowhere is the power asymmetry of this position itself questioned. Who decides what "capacity building" means? Who sets research agenda? The person seems to believe that good intentions and participatory structures suffice to overcome colonial patterns in science. That is naive.

2. **Systemic causes of climate crisis.** All actions operate within existing systems -- grants, parliamentary committees, EU regulation, corporate reporting. There is no action questioning fundamental economic structures -- no resistance against fossil corporations, no support for degrowth movements, no questioning of growth paradigm. The person acts as reformist optimist within a system that may not be reformable.

3. **Personal costs and sustainability of self.** 30 documented actions across 8 years show enormous workload: institute leadership, field research, policy advice, publication, teaching, community work -- simultaneously. Self-care, delegation, or questions of personal exhaustion appear nowhere. This blind spot is typical for mission-driven personalities and serious risk for entire project's sustainability.

4. **Impact measurement.** Much invested, much built, much published -- but actions contain almost no evidence for actual effect. Were monitoring stations used? Did regenerative agriculture persist? Did promoted early-career researchers actually build careers? The person seems to take output for impact -- a frequent mistake in science management.

## Op2 — Dimensional Rating

**D01 Sense of mission: 8**
Consistent thematic coherence across 8 years -- climate, justice, open science -- shows deep mission not messianically inflated but materialized in concrete structures.

**D02 Self-efficacy: 8**
Million-dollar budgets, UN testimony, policy influence at EU level, partnerships in 12 countries -- this person acts with clear self-understanding of being effective, without signs of omnipotence fantasy.

**D03 Work ethic: 9**
30 substantial actions in 8 years across all areas -- research, policy, technology, education, sustainability, investment -- suggest near-total identification with work with barely visible boundaries between person and assignment.

**D04 Sense of responsibility: 9**
Responsibility assumed in concentric circles: own institute (carbon-neutral), science system (open access, standards), Global South (capacity building), planet (climate policy). This is nearly comprehensive.

**D05 Techno-determinism: 4**
Technology consistently deployed (satellites, AI, sensors) but always as tool within social and institutional structures -- never as autonomous driver. Open source and local ownership show deliberately instrumental relationship.

**D06 Belief in progress: 7**
Actions throughout are constructive and solution-oriented -- no resignation, no alarmism for its own sake. But steady intensification (larger grants, broader networks, longer studies) reveals person knows it is not advancing fast enough.

**D07 Power concentration: 4**
Strong tendency toward distribution: open access, shared IP, capacity building, mentorship. Simultaneously the person exercises considerable decision power over budgets and agendas -- power is distributed but from a central position.

**D08 Urgency: 7**
Action frequency is high, ambitions grow steadily, themes (ocean acidification, coral reefs, emission reduction urgency) carry existential undertones. Yet patience for 10-year agreements and 5-year studies shows urgency does not tip into panic.

**D09 Human appreciation: 8**
Actions consistently show appreciation for human communities: indigenous sovereignty, early-career support, science communication, participatory research. People are partners, not objects. Slight reduction because actions focus more on groups/institutions than individuals.

**D10 Posthumanism: 2**
No hints of posthumanistic ambitions. Humans remain human -- goal is better cohabitation on a livable planet, not transcendence of the human.

**D11 Egalitarianism: 8**
Systematic redistribution of resources and knowledge toward underrepresented regions and groups. Simultaneously the person operates within existing hierarchies (institute director, policy advisor), somewhat limiting egalitarianism.

**D12 Locus of control: 7**
Clear conviction that action has effect -- visible in steady project expansion and partnership growth. But external dependence on policy decisions, funding, and international cooperation shows awareness that own control is limited.
